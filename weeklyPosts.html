Directed Field Work (DFW)

Corey Cherrington's work for the Celtic Arts Foundation (CAF)

I was able to start my hours as a Digital Library and Archives Intern for the Celtic Arts Foundation (CAF) just about two weeks before the Autumn 2021 quarter began. Before week one, my supervisor and I went over the expectations and workload for the project. The plan was that I would first evaluate the types of materials in the Robert Burns Dinner collection of CAF for whether they were present in both digital and physical format, whether they would have to be digitized of printed depending on what forms of the documents were manifested in the collection, and for the aboutness of the documents in the collection. Upon scanning through the digital and physical holdings in week one, I was able to set up a spreadsheet for inventorying the entire Burns Dinner Collection. Included in my spreadsheet were drop-down menus for the categories of material type and the categories requiring an answer to a yes/no or a present/absent question. These were represented in dropdown menus as Y/N and P/A respectively and were designated as the columns that would assist me in evaluating which materials needed to be digitized or printed.

After a first draft of the spreadsheet was created, I tested out my schema on the physical collection. When about ten resources had been catalogged with the schema, I discovered that my draft material type categories could be refined. For this part of the process, I took the material types I started out with and replaced vague or too specific terms with improved terms. For this, I applied my archival knowledge gained from my part-time job at the Gates Archive to select common material types, and checked these against Library of Congress and Getty's Art and Architeture thesaurus, the latter being a resource that my workplace commonly relies on for description of archival items. Using trusted thesauri in the field solidified my material type terms by week three. Additionally, I showed my spreadsheet to my supervisor to double check that I was on the right track. She suggested that I add new columns to the spreadsheet regarding the digitization process, and I did so at her instruction.

Weeks one and two were supplemented with research completed about the eventual online exhibition to be created using the Robert Burns Dinner collection and also the Highland Games collection. In this research, I began compiling relevant resources to later present to my host institution. These were saved in my University of Washington (UW) Google Drive and will be expanded upon throughout the quarter. In total, I went onsite to CAF once during week one.

Week 5 (October 11-17) was a very productive week in terms of new steps forward on the project. I started the week preparing to print the documents in my inventory list that met two requirements: 1.) the organization specified they wanted physical records of certain types, such as financial records, event programs, etc. 2.) The item was not in my inventory list for the current physical assets in this archive. I was able to complete printing all of documents meeting these requirements from 2001 through 2010. Next Wednesday, I will resume printing the remaining documents needing to be printed for the years 2011 through 2021. I was also able to create a system for giving printed items unique identification numbers. I recorded this workflow in my OneNote for Policies and Procedures for the CAF Archive. Additionally, I started recording steps for the CAF Archive metadata schema in the OneNote, created a chapter structure for various sections of the policies and procedures manual, and added other relevant details about CAF workflows and made these procedural decisions based on research I cited in the OneNote and also based on my own experience working for an archive.

I also found a platform that CAF can use for free up to 5GB that will allow them to display their archival holdings online for the public. The software, Preservica, also allows the organization to keep any assets private that they wish to keep private. Throughout the week, I experimented with Preservica for the purposes of evaluation for suitability for the CAF Archive and determined I should propose this software solution to my supervisors at CAF. I am extremely happy to report that this sorfware meets their organization's needs and that we will be using Preservica as a means to show the community the rich history of the organization. On the same day that this was decided, Sunday (10/17/2021), the organization received a new piece of technology that will make scanning projects much more straight-forward. The machine is called a Fujitsu ScanSnap, and my DFW supervisor, Elena, and I were able to test it out on a delecate music book. We found that the scanner produces clear images of scanned content and has the ability to correct scanned images and to perform Optical Character Recognition (OCR). This will be an immensely effective resource for ensuring accessibility of CAF's collections for the public.

Week 6: For this week, major landmarks include:

  1. I was able to scan and digitize every physical item in the Robert Burns Collection
  2. I started assigning unique catalog numbers to each uploaded scan
  3. I started separating the files by topic (based on our new controlled vocabulary whose primary authority source is the Getty Art and Architecture Thesaurus)
  4. I prepared to provide OCR for every scanned PDF from the physical collections
  5. Non-OCR PDFs in the digital collections were also sorted and prepared to obtain OCR capabilities
In a previous post, I noted that the ScanSnap should have direct to OCR abilities, and it does. However, it has not yet succeeded at making CAF's collection items have OCR, so I discussed this blocker with my internship coordinator and we decided that using Adobe Acrobat Reader DC (a software I already had on my computer) would be our best current option while we figure out how to make the new scanner scan items directly with OCR. The OCR capabilities would be added to all PDFs in CAF's collection the following week.

Week 7: This week was a successful week for completion of tasks that I had been preparing and planning for for a few weeks. I found quick and efficient ways to: give CAF's PDFs OCR readability in bulk and upload all collection items to Preservica in an already-organized folder structure I had been putting together for many weeks. Now that all items are uploaded to Preservica with their unique catalog numbers as placeholder titles, my next task is to add metadata to all uploaded collection items using the information I have already recorded about each item in my inventory list. I have also already simplified this process by connecting each inventory item to a unique catalog number, which should make this task go more smoothly. However, looking back at my inventory sheet, the scope and content notes that I have for most of the born-digital items are comparatively less substantive, and therefore less helpful for finding the content later. So throughout this process, I hope to expand the scope and content notes for the born-digital items

An important concept that I learned this week was that, when you put most of your time into planning and creating a structure for an archive, the time it takes later in the process to organize everything goes much quicker. I am also having so much fun working with this organization and the amazing people who are responsible for CAF's many years of success. Non-profits are awesome!

I should also note here that I have all collection items separated into a few specific content types: (1) Born-digital documents, (2) Digitized documents (3) Physical documents, including printed versions of relevant documents that were not yet in CAF's physical collection. All of these facets have been noted in my unique catalog numbering system as letters: DF for "Digital File," D for "Digitized," and F for "file (physical)" respectively. I have chosen these alphabetic indicators to begin each resource's catalog number and have explaned this in the Policies and Procedures manual I am writing for future archivists working with CAF. This practice was based on cataloging experience from my job as an archive intern elsewhere.
